🏢Business Customers — Definition & Types
Definition (Anderson & Narus): Business market customers are firms, institutions, or governments that acquire goods/services for their own use, to incorporate into products they produce, or for resale.

Why it matters: Unlike consumers who buy for personal satisfaction, business customers buy to create value or reduce costs in their own operations. This makes the purchase decision more rational, complex, and often involving multiple people — which is why B2B marketing requires a completely different approach than B2C.
  • B2B — OEM (Original Equipment Manufacturer): buys components to build own products (e.g., Land Rover buying parts) — Why: core competency is assembly/design, not making every part; cheaper and faster to source externally
  • B2B — User: buys to use in own operations (e.g., a factory buying machinery) — Why: the product directly enables or improves their internal processes
  • B2B — Intermediaries: buys to resell (distributors, retailers) — Why: suppliers don't want to invest in reaching every end customer themselves; intermediaries reduce distribution costs and complexity by acting as partners who already have the reach
  • B2B2C / B2R2C: sells to a business that then sells to consumers (e.g., Barry Callebaut → chocolate brands → consumers) — Why: the B2B supplier specializes in production at scale; the consumer-facing brand handles marketing, packaging, and retail
  • B2B2B: multiple business layers before end customer — Why: complex supply chains where each layer adds a specific capability or value (e.g., raw material → component → product → reseller)
  • B2G: sells to government institutions — Why: governments are massive buyers of infrastructure, IT, defense, and services; winning a government contract means large, stable, long-term revenue
  • Dual Marketing: same supplier targets both B2B and B2C channels simultaneously — Why: maximizes revenue by serving multiple market segments with the same or adapted product (e.g., Microsoft sells Office to businesses AND consumers)
Example — Digital Cinema Value System:
Texas Instruments (chips) → Barco (laser projectors) → CinemaNext (integrator) → Kinepolis (cinema) → End consumer
👥Decision Making Unit (DMU)

The DMU is the group of people involved in a B2B purchase decision. Each plays a distinct role:

DMU Roles — Who Is Involved & Why
🚨
Initiator — Head of Radiology
Recognizes the need — e.g., the current MRI is outdated, wait times are too long, or image quality is insufficient for diagnosis.
Why this role exists: someone inside the organization has to first say "we need this" before anything else can happen.
🛒
Buyer — Procurement / Purchasing Manager
Handles the formal process: sends out RFPs, negotiates price and contract terms, manages supplier relationships (Siemens, Philips, GE).
Why this role exists: clinical staff shouldn't be negotiating contracts — a dedicated buyer ensures the hospital gets the best deal and follows procurement policy.
✅
Decision Maker — Hospital CEO / Board of Directors
Gives final approval, especially for a multi-million euro capital investment. Focuses on budget, strategic fit, and long-term value.
Why this role exists: an MRI scanner can cost €1–3M+. No single department head has that authority — it must be signed off at the top.
🔗
Coordinator — Project Manager / IT & Facilities Manager
Coordinates across departments — ensures the room is built to spec, the IT infrastructure supports the scanner, installation is on schedule, and staff training is arranged.
Why this role exists: buying an MRI isn't just purchasing a machine — it involves construction, IT integration, safety compliance, and logistics. Someone needs to manage it all.
👩‍⚕️
Users — MRI Technicians & Radiologists
Operate the machine daily. Care about image quality, ease of use, software interface, scan speed, and patient comfort.
Why this role exists: they know exactly what works in practice. A machine that looks great on paper but is painful to use daily will cause problems — their input prevents bad purchases.
💬
Influencers — Medical Physicists, External Consultants & Peer Hospitals
Provide technical expertise — evaluate Tesla strength, magnet technology, software capabilities, radiation safety, and maintenance costs. Peer hospitals share real-world experiences.
Why this role exists: the procurement team and CEO can't evaluate technical specs alone. Expert opinion carries huge weight and can make or break a supplier's chances — even informally.
⚠️ Remember Bonoma: The radiologist (User) might have no formal authority, but if they refuse to work with a certain scanner, the purchase won't happen. Power ≠ rank.

📦Direct vs. Indirect Procurement — What's the Difference?
When a company buys something, there are always two fundamental questions: Does this go into what we sell? If yes — it's direct. If no — it's indirect. Both matter, but they require completely different strategies.
🔵 Direct Procurement
Buying raw materials, components, or services that go directly into the final product you sell to customers.

Examples:
• A car maker buying steel & tires
• Tesla buying battery cells & seats
• A food company buying ingredients

Why it matters: If direct procurement stops, production stops — and revenue stops. It's mission-critical.
🟠 Indirect Procurement
Buying goods and services that keep the business running but never end up in the product you sell.

Examples:
• Office supplies, IT software, laptops
• Cleaning services, utilities, security
• Marketing agencies, consultants, travel

Why it matters: Doesn't drive revenue directly, but without it operations grind to a halt.
💡 Simple test: Will this purchase ever reach the customer as part of what we sell? Yes → Direct. No → Indirect.
Tesla buying battery cells = Direct. Tesla buying office chairs = Indirect.

⚠️ The Seesaw Effect (why lowest price fails for direct procurement):
Cutting the purchase price of a direct material often increases costs elsewhere — more defects, slower delivery, extra quality checks, or supplier reliability issues. TCO thinking exists specifically to avoid this trap.
🔗 Link to Kraljic Matrix:
Direct procurement items tend to fall in the Strategic or Leverage quadrants (high profit impact). Indirect items often land in Non-critical or Bottleneck quadrants. The matrix helps prioritize how much attention and relationship investment each category deserves.
💰3 Purchasing Orientations
1. Lowest Price
Maximize power over suppliers; multi-sourcing; use electronic reverse auctions. Risk: seesaw effect — cost reductions in one area cause increases elsewhere.
2. Lowest Total Cost of Ownership (TCO)
TCO = all costs of acquisition + use + disposal/recycling. Uses Activity-Based Costing. Multi-year costs stated as NPV. Avoids the seesaw effect.
3. Best Value
Value = worth in monetary terms of economic, technical, service, and social benefits minus costs. "Price is what you pay, value is what you get." — Warren Buffett

📊Customer Value Model — 8 Steps
  • Step 1: List all value and price elements for your offering
  • Step 2: Determine the incumbent / next best alternative
  • Step 3: Evaluate points of parity (same), contention (disputed), and difference (clearly better)
  • Step 4: Identify which points of difference are relevant to customers
  • Step 5: Write a word equation for each relevant value element to quantify monetary worth
  • Step 6: List non-quantifiable elements as placeholders (+/–/?)
  • Step 7: Create a value model spreadsheet (Total Value – Differential Price = Value-in-Use)
  • Step 8: State your value proposition
Value-in-Use (VIU)
Superior benefits in excess of costs & price your customer gets by choosing you over the competitor.
VIU = (Vf – Va) – (Pf – Pa)
VIU Price
Indifference price at which customer has no preference between you and competitor.
VIU Price = Pa + (Vf – Va)

⚖️ Kritische Analyse — Beperkingen van deze studie
🔴 Opdrachtgeversbias
De studie is commissioned by Slack — Slack betaalt Forrester om de studie uit te voeren. Hoewel Forrester editoriale onafhankelijkheid claimt, is er een structureel belang om positieve resultaten te beklemtonen. Vergelijk: een farmabedrijf dat een studie betaalt over zijn eigen medicijn. Dit maakt de studie bruikbaar als richtlijn, maar niet als objectief bewijs.
🔴 Selectiebias in de steekproef
De 5 geïnterviewde bedrijven en 264 respondenten zijn allemaal al Slack-gebruikers die succesvol de adoptie hebben doorgemaakt. Bedrijven die Slack hebben geprobeerd en afgeschaft — of nooit de ROI hebben gehaald — zijn niet vertegenwoordigd. Dit is een klassiek geval van survivorship bias: je hoort alleen de succesverhalen.
🟡 "Productivity recapture" assumptie is aanvechtbaar
Forrester rekent dat 50% van de bespaard tijd effectief productief wordt ingezet. Maar in de praktijk verdwijnt vrijgekomen tijd vaak in andere vergaderingen, koffiepauzes of nieuwe Slack-notificaties. Er is geen empirisch bewijs dat precies 50% van herwonnen tijd leidt tot extra economische output. Dit is de grootste onzekerheid in de $1,8M productiviteitsberekening.
🟡 Microsoft Teams als echte incumbent ontbreekt
De studie definieert de next best alternative als "gefragmenteerde tools" (e-mail + chat + meetings apart). Maar voor de meeste bedrijven is de echte keuze Slack vs. Microsoft Teams — dat gratis meekomt met Microsoft 365 en voor veel bedrijven al beschikbaar is. De vergelijking met Teams zou de ROI aanzienlijk kleiner maken. De studie vermijdt deze vergelijking bewust.
🟢 Wat de studie wél goed doet
De TCO-aanpak is methodologisch correct: kosten omvatten niet alleen de licentie maar ook IT-arbeid en implementatie. De risicobijstellingen (–15% op productiviteit, –5% op incident management) tonen eerlijkheid over onzekerheid. De splitsing tussen kwantificeerbare voordelen en placeholders (+/–/?) volgt exact de Anderson & Narus-logica. En de composite organization-aanpak is een legitieme methode om privacy van respondenten te beschermen.
💡 Examentip: Als prof vraagt naar beperkingen van een Customer Value Model in de praktijk — dit is het perfecte voorbeeld. De methodologie klopt, maar de inputs (wie voert de studie uit, wie worden bevraagd, welke assumptie voor recapture) bepalen het resultaat. Garbage in = garbage out.

🔍Market Research in B2B — Purpose
The purpose of market research is to better understand the customer's jobs to be done — the need for which they "hire" a company's market offerings.   • Hard to identify / reach the decision maker — gatekeepers filter access; the real decision maker may be hidden

  • Engage the sales organization? — Sales reps have direct customer contact and hear problems firsthand, but may introduce bias

👁️Observation as Market Research — Brady Example

🧲Bosch: Blue Tools vs Green Tools — B2B vs B2C Product Strategy
🔵 Blue Tools — Professional (B2B)
Sold to construction professionals, tradespeople. Higher specs, more durable, more expensive. Example: GLM 50 C laser measure — precise, rugged, Bluetooth-enabled. Purchased for productivity on job sites. Decision is rational (ROI, spec compliance).
🟢 Green Tools — Consumer (B2C)

⚙️B2B Marketing Mix: Products — Key Characteristics
  • Technically complex products — require engineering expertise to specify, sell, and support
  • (Extreme) importance of after-sales service — downtime is costly; service contracts are often more valuable than the product itself
  • Purchase based on specifications — detailed technical requirements (RFQ/tender) drive the decision, not emotion or packaging
  • Variable / customized design — products are often adapted per customer (custom components, bespoke software, tailored formulations)
💡 Contrast with B2C: A consumer buys a product off the shelf. A B2B buyer negotiates specs, delivery, service levels, and often co-develops the product with the supplier.

💰B2B Marketing Mix: Price — Characteristics
  • Based on quotations — no list price on the shelf; each deal starts from a formal quote (RFQ process)
  • Different payment conditions — payment terms vary (e.g., 30/60/90 days net, partial advance payments, milestones)
  • Non-transparent and negotiable — competitors rarely know each other's actual prices; everything is negotiated bilaterally
  • Price not often used as a promotion instrument — unlike B2C (sales, discounts), B2B promotion is about relationships, demos, trade fairs, and value articulation — not price cuts
⚠️Traditional Pricing Approaches — Limitations
Cost-Plus Pricing
Price = Cost + Markup

Problems:
• Assumes cost structure is fully known (it rarely is)
• Does not allow for dynamic pricing
• Why should my internal costs affect what the customer pays?
• Ignores customer willingness to pay

Example: CostPlus Drug Company (Mark Cuban) — shows costs explicitly ($31.20 manufacturing + 15% markup + $3 pharmacy labor + $5 shipping = $39) while retail competitors charge $9,657.
Competition-Based Pricing
Price = match or undercut competitor's price

Problems:
• Cedes control of a critical aspect of strategy
• Forces you to follow a "price leader" — your fate is in their hands
• What is the competitor's actual price? (B2B prices are non-transparent)
• Starts a race to the bottom; destroys value

Result: Neither approach is optimal — both ignore what the customer actually values.

🚚B2B Marketing Mix: Distribution — Overview
Direct Channels
Supplier firm reaches customer firm directly, through the supplier's own sales forces and logistics systems.

Many connections needed (N×M relationships), but full control and direct relationship management.
Indirect Channels
Resellers and third-party providers are involved in serving the customer firm.

⚖️Marketplace Equity — Build Long-Term Channel Value
Marketplace Equity = the value a customer firm receives from buying a particular supplier's product or service through a particular reseller. It is the product of three equities:
  • Channel Equity — value a reseller receives from the channel offering a supplier provides (training, co-marketing, support, tools)
  • Reseller Equity — value of buying from this reseller vs. other authorized resellers in the local trade area (service, expertise, trust)
  • Brand Equity — value of buying this supplier's product vs. competing suppliers (brand strength, reputation, innovation)
💡 Strategic insight: Barco invests heavily in Channel Equity (partner programs, technical training, co-marketing funds) so that its resellers are better equipped, more motivated, and more loyal than competitors' resellers. This creates a sustainable marketplace advantage beyond just product specs.
🎯Distribution Channel Goals
  • Maximize market access — provide coverage and exposure to as many profitable customers as possible
  • Optimize value-added — meet customer requirements and satisfy preferences to create the greatest possible value for each market segment
  • Minimize cost-to-serve — minimize the total expenditures required to deliver the intended offerings to targeted segments and customers
Barco Clickshare — Push & Pull Strategy:
AV channel (resellers, integrators) = 80% of business. IT channel (VAR, SMB, DMRs) = 20%. Barco uses a mixed distribution design with different intermediary types for different customer segments. Simultaneously, Barco invests in Pull marketing (direct to end customers via market influencers, web lead generation, business development managers at F1000 companies) to create demand that flows down through channel partners.
🔄What is Derived Demand?

In B2B markets, demand for a supplier's product is not generated directly by the business customer — it is derived from the demand that the business customer's own customers (and ultimately end-consumers) have for their products.

B2B demand is ultimately a function of what consumers want and buy. A supplier three tiers up the supply chain must still understand and respond to shifts in end-consumer preferences.

Session objective: "Analyzing Derived Demand and its Implications" — understanding how consumer sentiment cascades upstream through the supply chain, and how B2B firms must monitor and react to end-consumer trends.
Key implication: Even if your direct customer is satisfied, a shift in their customers' preferences can destroy demand for your B2B product overnight — unless you proactively manage it.

Rather than waiting for its direct customers (bra manufacturers) to complain, Bekaert proactively monitored consumer sentiment and adapted its product. The strategic response had two parts:

1. Monitor consumer sentiment ↑ upstream
Bekaert began tracking social media and consumer review platforms — not because consumers were their direct customers, but because consumer preferences are the root cause of derived demand. This is the B2B2C insight: B2B companies need consumer intelligence even if they never sell to consumers directly.
2. Develop non-squeaking wire
Bekaert's R&D team redesigned the brassiere wire to eliminate the squeak — a material/coating innovation at the B2B level that directly addressed a consumer-level problem. This is a derived demand driven product innovation: consumer pain point → upstream component redesign.
✅ Result: By solving the consumer problem at the component level, Bekaert added value to its direct customers (bra manufacturers) who could now deliver a higher-quality end product — creating a competitive advantage for Bekaert in the supply chain.

🎯Strategic Implications for B2B Marketers
  • Monitor end-consumer sentiment even if consumers are not your direct customers — use social listening, market research at the consumer tier
  • Map your full value system from raw input to end consumer — understand where your product lives and what consumer trends could disrupt demand
  • Invest in B2B2C innovation — redesign your B2B component/service to eliminate consumer pain points before your direct customers' competitors do it first
  • Engage consumers directly when possible (pull strategy) — Bekaert could invest in "Bekaert Inside" branding (like Intel Inside) to create consumer pull for bras with Bekaert wire
  • Diversify across value systems — Bekaert operates in road safety barriers, diamond wire saws, natural stone cutting, brassiere wire; each with its own end-consumer demand curve, reducing overall derived demand volatility
  • Beaulieu International Group (from our factory visit) is also exposed to derived demand: polymer fibres and flooring materials are incorporated into consumer-facing products — consumer design trends (e.g. away from carpets to LVT) directly shift Beaulieu's B2B orders
⚡The Core Shift: From Traditional to New B2B Approach

The strategic response to derived demand requires a fundamental rethinking of how B2B companies position themselves in the value chain. Instead of only pushing product/service downstream, they must also pull information and intelligence upstream from end-consumers.

Traditional B2B Approach
B  →  B¹  →  C
  • Product/service flows downstream only
  • No interaction between B2B firm and end-consumer
  • No/indirect insight on consumer markets and preferences
New B2B Approach (Strategic Response)
B  ⟵  ·····  B¹  ····· ⟵  C
  • Engage with end-consumers to improve processes
  • Sell to consumer segments (direct or indirect)
  • Digitally-enhance business/consumer products/services
  • Offer or sell digital business/consumer products/services
🔑 Key insight (Accenture Digital / Muylle et al.): The dotted arrow in the new B2B approach represents information and intelligence flowing back from consumer (C) to the upstream B2B firm (B). This is the strategic response — actively closing the feedback loop that derived demand creates.
📈Derived Demand Effects to Understand Before Responding
⚡ Acceleration Effect A small change in consumer demand can create a disproportionately large swing in upstream B2B demand. EV adoption → massive race for lithium, cobalt and nickel (prices up 190%+ in 18 months). Carmakers locked in long-term supply agreements from mining companies as consumer uptake forecasts increased.
⏱ Time-Lag Effect Derived demand changes hit B2B suppliers with a delay. Boeing 737 MAX grounding (2019) and COVID-19 didn't immediately destroy Solvay's orders — production pipelines buffer the signal — but the impact eventually arrived with full force. Apple slashed production orders for all three iPhone models, only after demand shortfall was confirmed downstream.
⚖️ Passing the Blame When end-consumer products fail, the blame can travel upstream to B2B suppliers. VW diesel emissions scandal → Bosch (engine software supplier) was implicated and faced cascading legal claims. Being an invisible B2B supplier does not protect against reputational and legal derived-demand risk.
Supply-side shocks also create derived demand ripples: Madagascar cyclone → vanilla pod shortage → ice cream makers scramble for supply → price spike hits end consumers. B2B suppliers must monitor both demand-side and supply-side shocks anywhere in their extended value chain.
🏗️Strategic Response 1: Design for Customers of Customers

Rather than designing products that satisfy your direct B2B customer's specs, design for what the end-consumer actually experiences. The end-consumer's voice now travels upstream via social media — B2B suppliers can tap this directly.

✈️ Airbus — "The Cabin Social Media Built"
Airbus designed its new "Airspace" cabin concept by analysing what flying passengers were saying on social media — comparing one seat vs. another, one airline vs. another. Direct customers (airlines) buy the planes, but Airbus listened to the passengers. The result: a redesigned cabin that helps airlines sell more tickets, creating stronger derived demand for Airbus aircraft.
🍫 Bekaert — Non-Squeaking Brassiere Wire
Bekaert monitored Twitter complaints from consumers about their bras squeaking. Though consumers never mentioned Bekaert, the component-level cause was clear. Bekaert redesigned the wire to eliminate the squeak — a B2B product innovation directly addressing a consumer pain point that the direct customer (bra manufacturer) had not explicitly requested.
🏷️Strategic Response 2: Ingredient Branding (End-User Marketing)

B2B suppliers can create consumer pull by building a brand that consumers recognise and request — even though they buy the finished product from a B2B2C intermediary. This is called ingredient branding.

🖥️ Intel Inside / GORE-TEX / Shimano
"Intel Inside" is the canonical example: Intel is a B2B chip supplier, but consumers ask "does this laptop have Intel?" — creating pull that forces PC manufacturers to keep using Intel. Same for GORE-TEX (textiles) and Shimano (bicycle gears). Ingredient brands work best when there are many OEMs, few suppliers, and the component is complex and important to the final product.
🎬 HDR by Barco — Cinema Ingredient Branding
Barco (B2B cinema projector supplier) launched consumer-facing "HDR by Barco" premium format — promoted directly to moviegoers via Forbes reviews and co-branded with major film launches (Gladiator II). Cinema chains select Barco projectors partly because consumers actively seek out "HDR by Barco" screenings, creating derived demand pull from consumer to cinema (B¹) to Barco (B).
Ingredient Branding Likelihood of Success (Kotler):
① High number of OEMs + Low number of suppliers → ingredient branding feasible
② High complexity of component relative to final product + High importance for functionality → ingredient branding feasible
Both conditions: Intel (microprocessors), GORE-TEX (textile laminates), Shimano (bicycle gears)
📊Strategic Response 3: Measure NPS / Consumer Satisfaction

B2B companies can proactively measure consumer satisfaction with the end product — even if they don't sell to consumers directly — using Net Promoter Score (NPS) and similar tools. This gives them leading indicators of derived demand shifts before they show up in order volumes.

🚗 Carglass (Belron)
Carglass installs windscreens — B2B supplier (adhesive, glass) but B2C service. They measure consumer NPS obsessively to understand how the end-customer perceives the repair experience. This consumer intelligence feeds back to improve the B2B product (adhesive formulas, installation tools) and service standards upstream.
☕ illy / D&L Sauces — Place Strategy
illy coffee (B2B supplier to café chains) and D&L sauces (B2B supplier to chip shops) both created consumer-facing apps/maps showing where consumers can find their products. By understanding where consumers consume their product, they feed intelligence back to improve distribution (Place) decisions — a digital consumer engagement tool for a B2B2C product.
🔗Strategic Response 4: Traceability as Competitive Advantage

In the age of "passing the blame," traceability transforms from a compliance obligation into a strategic asset. B2B suppliers who can prove exactly which components went where — and that they met standards — are protected from derived-demand scandals and can charge premium pricing.

🍾 Bekaert — Champagne Cork Wire (Bekilacq®)
Bekaert offers lacquered low-carbon steel wire for champagne cork hoods with full traceability. The product's coating formula is documented to provide corrosion resistance and superior machinery output. If a champagne producer faces a consumer complaint about cork quality, Bekaert can prove exactly what its wire delivered — no "passing the blame" risk.
🚗 VW/Bosch Diesel Scandal — The Risk of NOT Having Traceability
Bosch supplied engine software to Volkswagen. When the emissions scandal broke, Bosch faced cascading legal claims as a B2B supplier implicated in the consumer-facing scandal. Clear documentation of what was delivered, what was instructed by VW, and what Bosch's role was became essential to limit legal liability — traceability as a defensive strategic tool.

💡Why Engage Consumers? — Benefits for B2B Firms
  • More revenues through increased sales volume — stimulating primary (derived) demand for the end product means more business customers need your B2B component
  • Price premiums driven by preference insights — knowing what consumers prefer allows B2B firms to create differentiated products that command higher prices along the chain
  • Higher customer switching costs — when a B2B firm is embedded in the consumer experience (via app, data, branding), business customers are reluctant to switch suppliers
  • Cost efficiencies in internal operations — better understanding of end-consumer markets improves inventory management, production planning, and logistics (demand forecasting)

🔢The 6 Types — Detailed Definitions
① Process Improvement (B2B, Existing product line)
  • Develop internal business intelligence from consumer data to optimize internal operations
  • Analyze consumer product preferences for product configuration and discovery of new applications
  • Generate derived demand — by educating consumers, you pull demand toward your business customers' products
  • Generate and qualify leads — consumer interactions reveal which businesses don't yet use your product
  • Optimize pricing — consumer data reveals what different segments value and how much they pay
② Digitally-Enhanced Business Product (B2B, Digitally-enhanced line)
Make existing B2B products more appealing to business customers by introducing information features, information processing, personalization or digital functionality based on consumer data. The product remains B2B — it's sold to business customers — but consumer engagement makes it better.
③ Digital Business Product (B2B, Digital line)
Develop and sell entirely new information or software applications based on consumer data to business customers. This is a new product category — not a physical product, but a data/insight product sold B2B.
④ Consumer Sales (B2C, Existing product line)
Leverage digital technology to sell the existing (or adapted) product online to consumers directly (own e-commerce site) or indirectly (via Amazon, marketplace). The B2B firm becomes a B2C seller.
⑤ Digitally-Enhanced Consumer Product (B2C, Digitally-enhanced line)
Enhance the consumer product by adding information features, processing, personalization, or digital functionality — typically via a mobile app. Consumer gets a richer product experience; the B2B firm gains consumer data.
⑥ Digital Consumer Product (B2C, Digital line)
Develop and sell entirely new information or software applications based on consumer data directly to consumers. A brand-new product line for the B2B firm — pure digital, B2C.
☕Applied Example — Industrial Coffee Machine Manufacturer
Context: A B2B organization sells coffee machines and coffee beans to offices, bars, hotels, and restaurants (business customers), but not directly to consumers. How can digital technology let them engage consumers across all 6 framework types?
B2B Quadrant — Engaging consumers for business benefit
①
Process Improvement
  • Inform consumers where to find their coffee machines (website/app) → generates derived demand: consumers ask coffee shops for "that machine" → shop buys more from the B2B firm
  • Monitor social media about coffee consumption → identifies shops without the machine as new customer leads
  • Interact with consumers on coffee brands/flavors → adapts product offering to consumer preferences → indirectly increases sales at coffee shops (business customers)
②
Digitally-Enhanced Business Product
Mobile app for consumers to customize and save their coffee composition, track and monitor consumption. Coffee shops equipped with app-connected machines become more appealing to consumers (same coffee every time, consumption tracking). Shops without the app are at a disadvantage → incentivizes business customers to upgrade/buy the machine.
③
Digital Business Product
Aggregated consumer data from the app (consumption insights, consumer profiles, time of consumption) is packaged as a data product sold to business customers (bars, hotels, corporate clients). Enables them to better tailor their coffee offerings and optimize stock. A new B2B revenue stream from consumer intelligence.
B2C Quadrant — Directly serving consumers
④
Consumer Sales
Adapt the industrial coffee machine for the home consumer market and sell it direct (own e-commerce website) or indirect (via Amazon). This is a full channel shift — the B2B firm becomes a B2C retailer for this product line.
⑤
Digitally-Enhanced Consumer Product
Mobile app for home consumers: customize coffee composition, track consumption, receive maintenance/repair alerts, re-order capsules when out of stock. Enhances the physical machine with a digital layer — increasing stickiness and repeat purchases.
⑥
Digital Consumer Product
Based on energy-level tracking after coffee consumption, the app recommends when to drink which type of coffee for optimal energy and health impact. Based on preferences of consumers with similar tastes, it proposes other coffee blends. A fully new digital product sold to consumers — generating new revenue from data.

🌐What is a Digital Platform?

"A digital platform is an intermediary that facilitates interactions between and among stakeholders such as consumers, application providers and partners." Basu & Muylle, 2023

  • Serves one or more sets of participants: one-sided or multi-sided (customers and producers)
  • Involves multiple types of producers: advertisers, developers, merchants
  • Provides value through the resources and products of its participants
  • Offers mechanisms that enable participants to find, interact, collaborate and/or transact more effectively on the platform than without it
🗂️3 Types of Digital Platforms (Basu & Muylle, 2023)
💡 Innovation Platform

Leverages wisdom of crowds. Facilitates intellectual input on problems from many participants; enables collaboration and valuable synergies.

Example: online forum for a special interest group

🤝 Engagement Platform

Enables large numbers of participants to mutually interact and share common interests.

Examples: X (Twitter), Roblox, social networking sites, gaming platforms

🛒 Transaction Platform

Brings together producers/sellers and consumers. Digital equivalent of a marketplace or mall.

Example: Amazon Marketplaces

Combinations exist: Facebook is simultaneously an Engagement + Transaction + Innovation platform

🔗Network Effects (= Network Externalities)

The value of a platform increases as more participants join. Each new user creates value for all existing users — this is the core growth engine of platforms.

TypeDefinitionDirectionExample
Same-side positive More users on one side → more value for that same side Side A → Side A More buyers on Amazon → more competition, better prices for buyers
Same-side negative More users on one side → less value for that side Side A → Side A ↓ More sellers → more competition among sellers (bad for sellers)
Cross-side positive More users on one side → more value for the other side Side A → Side B ↑ More buyers → attracts more sellers; more sellers → attracts more buyers
Cross-side negative More users on one side → less value for the other side Side A → Side B ↓ More advertisers → more ads → worse experience for users
🔒Lock-in Effects & Multi-homing
Lock-in Effect

Strong network effects create switching costs. The more entrenched a platform becomes, the harder it is for users to leave — even if a better alternative exists.

Example: Your social graph on Facebook/LinkedIn — leaving means losing connections

Multi-homing

Participating in multiple platforms simultaneously. Reduces lock-in. Platform challenge: keep users from multi-homing by offering exclusive value.

Example: Sellers listing on both Amazon and Bol.com

🐔The Chicken-and-Egg Problem

A core challenge for cross-side network effects: to attract buyers you need sellers, but to attract sellers you need buyers. Which comes first?

Strategy 1 — Subsidize one side: Make one side free or pay them to join first. Amazon subsidized buyers with low prices and free shipping. Credit card companies charge merchants, not cardholders.
Strategy 2 — Build a single-user tool first: Launch something valuable even with zero network. OpenTable was a restaurant management tool before becoming a reservation platform.
Strategy 3 — Simulate one side: Platform acts as the supply side until enough real suppliers join. PayPal seeded eBay listings by pretending to be buyers.
Critical mass: Once enough users are on both sides, the platform becomes self-sustaining and growth accelerates (flywheel effect).

🎯Assessing a Marketplace Opportunity (Basu & Muylle, 2023)

Why would an established company launch a marketplace? Opportunity is large when:

  • Number of current and potential participants on each side is large (high market fragmentation)
  • Resources of individual participants are limited — they need the platform's infrastructure
  • There is clear value in interactions on each side of the platform
  • The firm can offer a set of core capabilities to each side, based on a common technological core
Strategic logic for incumbents: A marketplace allows a company to expand its product/service catalogue without bearing inventory risk, monetize existing traffic and trust, and collect valuable data on buyer behaviour across a wider range of categories.

The Challenge
  • TF could only address 10–80% of each customer's product needs — leaving a large assortment gap
  • Warehouse capacity constraints prevented stocking all products
  • Goal: become a "one-stop shop" — sole supplier for all customer needs
  • Solution: launch a marketplace in April 2022 (built on Mirakl platform)

  • Familiar experience: same webshop interface, same product categories — no learning curve
  • Better discovery: detailed descriptions, ability to contact sellers before ordering, related/complementary product suggestions
  • Personalized: webshop tailored to each of the 14 customer categories

Value to SELLERS (producers/brands)
  • Instant reach: direct access to 10,000 established B2B customers across Belgium & Luxembourg
  • Launch visibility: welcome package — newsletter promotion, online banner, inclusion in monthly paper catalogue
  • Trusted context: TF's brand reputation reduces buyer hesitation toward new sellers

  • Coaching: TF coaches sellers on pricing, product data, and performance metrics
  • Low risk: €40/month subscription only charged in active months
  • Sales force of 65 reps (access to tablets planned) as an additional sales channel

🔗Q2 — Network Effects of the TF Marketplace

The TF marketplace is a two-sided platform with sellers and customers as the two sides. Network effects arise in four quadrants:

✅ SAME-SIDE POSITIVE
  • Customers → Customers: More customers using the marketplace → TF gains richer purchasing data → better personalization and product recommendations for all customers
  • Sellers → Sellers: More reputable sellers on TF → raises the overall quality standard → motivates other sellers to improve performance to stay listed
❌ SAME-SIDE NEGATIVE
  • Sellers → Sellers: More sellers in the same category → more price competition → lower margins per seller. A night shop supplier competes with new entrants in snacks/candy.
  • Customers → Customers: Two competing convenience stores in the same area both use the marketplace → same competitive products available to rivals → reduced differentiation
↔️ CROSS-SIDE POSITIVE
  • More sellers → Customers: Wider assortment → TF becomes a true one-stop shop → more value for customers → customers order more → platform grows
  • More customers → Sellers: Larger customer base → more sales opportunities → more attractive for sellers to join → more revenue per seller
  • This cross-side flywheel is TF's core growth engine
↔️ CROSS-SIDE NEGATIVE
  • Poor sellers → Customers: Sellers with late delivery, high refund rates, or wrong products damage customer trust in the whole marketplace — reputation spills over to TF brand
  • Late-paying customers → Sellers: Customers who miss the 30-day payment deadline harm sellers' cash flow → sellers become reluctant to join or fulfill small orders
Key insight: The suspension criteria (order acceptance >80%, refund rate <20%, incident rate <12%, etc.) are TF's mechanism to contain cross-side negative effects — protecting customers from bad sellers before harm spreads.

💡Q4 — Suggestions for Improving Seller & Customer Experience
The following ideas address the specific friction points identified in the case — prioritized by impact and feasibility given TF's limited marketplace team.
For Customers
  • Marketplace access on sales rep tablets: Reps can then demonstrate, recommend, and place marketplace orders during customer visits — removing the biggest promotion gap
  • Unified delivery via cross-docking: Merge marketplace shipments into TF's own delivery routes where geographically feasible → customers receive one combined order instead of multiple separate deliveries
  • Customer review & rating system: Allows buyers to see seller reliability before ordering → reduces risk, builds trust, incentivizes sellers to perform
  • Unified payment flow: Customer pays TF (as usual), TF pays seller → eliminates the confusion of paying an unknown seller directly
  • AI-powered recommendations: "Customers like you also ordered X from the marketplace" — leverages TF's data advantage for cross-sell
For Sellers
  • Payment guarantee / insurance scheme: TF guarantees payment to sellers (and collects from the customer) — removes the biggest barrier to onboarding and eliminates non-payment risk
  • Automated product data onboarding: Expand aggregator feed connections and provide better API templates → reduce the #1 operational friction sellers face
  • Tiered seller programme: "Gold" sellers (meeting all KPIs) get extra visibility in search, newsletter features, and lower commission → incentivizes quality and loyalty
  • Seller dashboard with customer insights: Share anonymized data on which customer categories buy which products → helps sellers optimize their catalogue and pricing for TF's audience
  • Proactive coaching: KPI-triggered coaching sessions (when a seller approaches suspension thresholds) rather than waiting for failure → prevents account suspensions
Priority recommendation: The single highest-impact improvement is giving sales reps marketplace access on their tablets. With 65 reps visiting customers daily, this turns every customer visit into a marketplace promotion opportunity — at near-zero additional cost. It directly addresses both the customer discovery gap and the seller visibility problem simultaneously.
