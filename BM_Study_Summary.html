Foundational Ideas

"The purpose of business is to create a customer. The business enterprise has two — and only two — basic functions: marketing and innovation. Marketing and innovation produce results; all the rest are costs."

— Peter Drucker

"Marketing is the science and art of exploring, creating, and delivering value to satisfy the needs of a target market at a profit."

— Philip Kotler
📌Core Takeaways
  • A business exists to create customers — marketing is not a department, it's the core purpose
  • Marketing = exploring + creating + delivering value profitably
  • B2B (Business-to-Business) marketing targets organizations, not individual consumers
  • B2B and B2C differ in degree, not in kind — but those degrees are substantial (Webster)
  • Many of the world's most valuable brands operate in both B2B and B2C (e.g., Microsoft, Amazon, Samsung)

🏢Business Customers — Definition & Types
Definition (Anderson & Narus): Business market customers are firms, institutions, or governments that acquire goods/services for their own use, to incorporate into products they produce, or for resale.

Why it matters: Unlike consumers who buy for personal satisfaction, business customers buy to create value or reduce costs in their own operations. This makes the purchase decision more rational, complex, and often involving multiple people — which is why B2B marketing requires a completely different approach than B2C.
  • B2B — OEM (Original Equipment Manufacturer): buys components to build own products (e.g., Land Rover buying parts) — Why: core competency is assembly/design, not making every part; cheaper and faster to source externally
  • B2B — User: buys to use in own operations (e.g., a factory buying machinery) — Why: the product directly enables or improves their internal processes
  • B2B — Intermediaries: buys to resell (distributors, retailers) — Why: suppliers don't want to invest in reaching every end customer themselves; intermediaries reduce distribution costs and complexity by acting as partners who already have the reach
  • B2B2C / B2R2C: sells to a business that then sells to consumers (e.g., Barry Callebaut → chocolate brands → consumers) — Why: the B2B supplier specializes in production at scale; the consumer-facing brand handles marketing, packaging, and retail
  • B2B2B: multiple business layers before end customer — Why: complex supply chains where each layer adds a specific capability or value (e.g., raw material → component → product → reseller)
  • B2G: sells to government institutions — Why: governments are massive buyers of infrastructure, IT, defense, and services; winning a government contract means large, stable, long-term revenue
  • Dual Marketing: same supplier targets both B2B and B2C channels simultaneously — Why: maximizes revenue by serving multiple market segments with the same or adapted product (e.g., Microsoft sells Office to businesses AND consumers)
Example — Digital Cinema Value System:
Texas Instruments (chips) → Barco (laser projectors) → CinemaNext (integrator) → Kinepolis (cinema) → End consumer
👥Decision Making Unit (DMU)

The DMU is the group of people involved in a B2B purchase decision. Each plays a distinct role:

RoleKey Responsibilities
BuyerPre-qualifies suppliers (gatekeeper), negotiates terms, evaluates suppliers. Increasingly strategic role.
GatekeeperControls information flow to/from suppliers; filters who gets access
InfluencerInfluences decisions for strategic goals; can be internal or external experts (consultants); formal or informal
UserActually uses the product/service; often involved in performance feedback
Decision MakerHas final authority on the purchase
⚠️ Key insight (Bonoma): Power does not correlate perfectly with organizational rank. Those with little formal power may block or hinder a purchase.
🏥DMU Example — Buying an MRI Scanner
An MRI scanner is a classic B2B purchase: expensive, complex, long decision cycle, and involves many people across the hospital. Here's how the DMU typically looks — and why each role exists.
Customer Segments (who might buy an MRI scanner)
🏥
Public Hospital
Budget-driven, long approval process, government funding constraints
🏢
Private Clinic
ROI-focused, faster decisions, willing to pay for premium features
🔬
Research Institute
Needs advanced specs, grant-funded, heavily influenced by academic experts
DMU Roles — Who Is Involved & Why
🚨
Initiator — Head of Radiology
Recognizes the need — e.g., the current MRI is outdated, wait times are too long, or image quality is insufficient for diagnosis.
Why this role exists: someone inside the organization has to first say "we need this" before anything else can happen.
🛒
Buyer — Procurement / Purchasing Manager
Handles the formal process: sends out RFPs, negotiates price and contract terms, manages supplier relationships (Siemens, Philips, GE).
Why this role exists: clinical staff shouldn't be negotiating contracts — a dedicated buyer ensures the hospital gets the best deal and follows procurement policy.
✅
Decision Maker — Hospital CEO / Board of Directors
Gives final approval, especially for a multi-million euro capital investment. Focuses on budget, strategic fit, and long-term value.
Why this role exists: an MRI scanner can cost €1–3M+. No single department head has that authority — it must be signed off at the top.
🔗
Coordinator — Project Manager / IT & Facilities Manager
Coordinates across departments — ensures the room is built to spec, the IT infrastructure supports the scanner, installation is on schedule, and staff training is arranged.
Why this role exists: buying an MRI isn't just purchasing a machine — it involves construction, IT integration, safety compliance, and logistics. Someone needs to manage it all.
👩‍⚕️
Users — MRI Technicians & Radiologists
Operate the machine daily. Care about image quality, ease of use, software interface, scan speed, and patient comfort.
Why this role exists: they know exactly what works in practice. A machine that looks great on paper but is painful to use daily will cause problems — their input prevents bad purchases.
💬
Influencers — Medical Physicists, External Consultants & Peer Hospitals
Provide technical expertise — evaluate Tesla strength, magnet technology, software capabilities, radiation safety, and maintenance costs. Peer hospitals share real-world experiences.
Why this role exists: the procurement team and CEO can't evaluate technical specs alone. Expert opinion carries huge weight and can make or break a supplier's chances — even informally.
⚠️ Remember Bonoma: The radiologist (User) might have no formal authority, but if they refuse to work with a certain scanner, the purchase won't happen. Power ≠ rank.
🔗 Link to Buying Process: For a hospital buying its first MRI (New Task), all 8 buying steps are followed. For an upgrade from an existing brand (Modified Rebuy), steps 1–6 are shortened. For a routine maintenance contract renewal (Straight Rebuy), it goes straight to Order.

📦Direct vs. Indirect Procurement — What's the Difference?
When a company buys something, there are always two fundamental questions: Does this go into what we sell? If yes — it's direct. If no — it's indirect. Both matter, but they require completely different strategies.
🔵 Direct Procurement
Buying raw materials, components, or services that go directly into the final product you sell to customers.

Examples:
• A car maker buying steel & tires
• Tesla buying battery cells & seats
• A food company buying ingredients

Why it matters: If direct procurement stops, production stops — and revenue stops. It's mission-critical.
🟠 Indirect Procurement
Buying goods and services that keep the business running but never end up in the product you sell.

Examples:
• Office supplies, IT software, laptops
• Cleaning services, utilities, security
• Marketing agencies, consultants, travel

Why it matters: Doesn't drive revenue directly, but without it operations grind to a halt.
💡 Simple test: Will this purchase ever reach the customer as part of what we sell? Yes → Direct. No → Indirect.
Tesla buying battery cells = Direct. Tesla buying office chairs = Indirect.

⚠️ The Seesaw Effect (why lowest price fails for direct procurement):
Cutting the purchase price of a direct material often increases costs elsewhere — more defects, slower delivery, extra quality checks, or supplier reliability issues. TCO thinking exists specifically to avoid this trap.
🔗 Link to Kraljic Matrix:
Direct procurement items tend to fall in the Strategic or Leverage quadrants (high profit impact). Indirect items often land in Non-critical or Bottleneck quadrants. The matrix helps prioritize how much attention and relationship investment each category deserves.
💰3 Purchasing Orientations
1. Lowest Price
Maximize power over suppliers; multi-sourcing; use electronic reverse auctions. Risk: seesaw effect — cost reductions in one area cause increases elsewhere.
2. Lowest Total Cost of Ownership (TCO)
TCO = all costs of acquisition + use + disposal/recycling. Uses Activity-Based Costing. Multi-year costs stated as NPV. Avoids the seesaw effect.
3. Best Value
Value = worth in monetary terms of economic, technical, service, and social benefits minus costs. "Price is what you pay, value is what you get." — Warren Buffett

📊Customer Value Model — 8 Steps
  • Step 1: List all value and price elements for your offering
  • Step 2: Determine the incumbent / next best alternative
  • Step 3: Evaluate points of parity (same), contention (disputed), and difference (clearly better)
  • Step 4: Identify which points of difference are relevant to customers
  • Step 5: Write a word equation for each relevant value element to quantify monetary worth
  • Step 6: List non-quantifiable elements as placeholders (+/–/?)
  • Step 7: Create a value model spreadsheet (Total Value – Differential Price = Value-in-Use)
  • Step 8: State your value proposition
Value-in-Use (VIU)
Superior benefits in excess of costs & price your customer gets by choosing you over the competitor.
VIU = (Vf – Va) – (Pf – Pa)
VIU Price
Indifference price at which customer has no preference between you and competitor.
VIU Price = Pa + (Vf – Va)

🌍Real-World Toepassing — Forrester TEI studie: Slack voor technische teams (2023)
Achtergrond & Context
🏢 Over Slack
Slack is een zakelijk communicatieplatform opgericht in 2013, initieel als interne tool voor een gamingbedrijf (Tiny Speck). In 2021 overgenomen door Salesforce voor $27,7 miljard — een van de grootste software-overnames ooit. Slack positioneert zich als "het hoofdkwartier voor hybride werken": één platform voor berichten, bestanden, workflows en app-integraties. De belangrijkste concurrenten zijn Microsoft Teams (gratis inbegrepen bij Microsoft 365, het grootste voordeel) en Google Chat.
📊 Over de Forrester TEI-methode
Forrester's Total Economic Impact (TEI) is een gestandaardiseerde methode om de ROI van IT-investeringen te berekenen. De studie werd in opdracht van Slack uitgevoerd — belangrijk kritisch punt. Forrester interviewde 5 bedrijven en bevroeg 264 respondenten. De resultaten werden samengevat in één composite organization: een fictief bedrijf van 2.000 medewerkers, 300 technische gebruikers en $500M omzet — representatief voor de steekproef, maar niet één specifiek bedrijf.
Waarom een "composite organization"?
Omdat geen enkel individueel bedrijf al zijn cijfers publiek wil delen, middelt Forrester de ervaringen van alle respondenten samen tot één modelorganisatie. Dit maakt de studie generaliseerbaar maar ook abstracter — de cijfers zijn representatief voor een gemiddeld technologiebedrijf van dit formaat, niet voor elke specifieke situatie.
Customer Value Model — Toegepast
Forrester paste exact dezelfde logica toe als Anderson & Narus. Next best alternative = gefragmenteerde combinatie van e-mail + aparte chat + meetingtools.

⚖️ Kritische Analyse — Beperkingen van deze studie
🔴 Opdrachtgeversbias
De studie is commissioned by Slack — Slack betaalt Forrester om de studie uit te voeren. Hoewel Forrester editoriale onafhankelijkheid claimt, is er een structureel belang om positieve resultaten te beklemtonen. Vergelijk: een farmabedrijf dat een studie betaalt over zijn eigen medicijn. Dit maakt de studie bruikbaar als richtlijn, maar niet als objectief bewijs.
🔴 Selectiebias in de steekproef
De 5 geïnterviewde bedrijven en 264 respondenten zijn allemaal al Slack-gebruikers die succesvol de adoptie hebben doorgemaakt. Bedrijven die Slack hebben geprobeerd en afgeschaft — of nooit de ROI hebben gehaald — zijn niet vertegenwoordigd. Dit is een klassiek geval van survivorship bias: je hoort alleen de succesverhalen.
🟡 "Productivity recapture" assumptie is aanvechtbaar
Forrester rekent dat 50% van de bespaard tijd effectief productief wordt ingezet. Maar in de praktijk verdwijnt vrijgekomen tijd vaak in andere vergaderingen, koffiepauzes of nieuwe Slack-notificaties. Er is geen empirisch bewijs dat precies 50% van herwonnen tijd leidt tot extra economische output. Dit is de grootste onzekerheid in de $1,8M productiviteitsberekening.
🟡 Microsoft Teams als echte incumbent ontbreekt
De studie definieert de next best alternative als "gefragmenteerde tools" (e-mail + chat + meetings apart). Maar voor de meeste bedrijven is de echte keuze Slack vs. Microsoft Teams — dat gratis meekomt met Microsoft 365 en voor veel bedrijven al beschikbaar is. De vergelijking met Teams zou de ROI aanzienlijk kleiner maken. De studie vermijdt deze vergelijking bewust.
🟢 Wat de studie wél goed doet
De TCO-aanpak is methodologisch correct: kosten omvatten niet alleen de licentie maar ook IT-arbeid en implementatie. De risicobijstellingen (–15% op productiviteit, –5% op incident management) tonen eerlijkheid over onzekerheid. De splitsing tussen kwantificeerbare voordelen en placeholders (+/–/?) volgt exact de Anderson & Narus-logica. En de composite organization-aanpak is een legitieme methode om privacy van respondenten te beschermen.
💡 Examentip: Als prof vraagt naar beperkingen van een Customer Value Model in de praktijk — dit is het perfecte voorbeeld. De methodologie klopt, maar de inputs (wie voert de studie uit, wie worden bevraagd, welke assumptie voor recapture) bepalen het resultaat. Garbage in = garbage out.
💬5 Value Propositions (Kotler)
  • More for More — premium quality at premium price
  • More for the Same — better quality, same price
  • More for Less — best deal (hard to sustain)
  • The Same for Less — equivalent quality, lower price
  • Less for Much Less — fewer features, much cheaper (e.g., budget options)
🌱Responsible Sourcing
  • Ethical and sustainable procurement practices across the supply chain
  • Example: Barry Callebaut's "Forever Chocolate" programme — commits to 100% sustainable chocolate by addressing child labour, deforestation, farmer poverty, and health
  • Typically formalized through a supplier Code of Conduct
